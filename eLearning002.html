2  Economic Models

During our course we will derive some conclusions or theorems which allow us to describe the real economy. But the immense complexity of the real economy makes it impossible for us to understand all the interrelationships. Therefore it is necessary to pick out the primary factors and relationships relevant to our problem. Hence we have to look at an abstraction from the real world.

Such an abstraction can be made with help of a model. A model is a simplified representation of the world. It is a formal method to analyse interactions and to draw conclusions. We will discuss some microeconomic models which will help us to understand why people are acting as they do in the real world. Of course, as all our models are just a simplification, our models will not allow to predict all interrelationships, but our models will be general enough to draw conclusions for understanding the real economy.

Furthermore it should be noted that a model should be applicalbe in general and not only in a special case and the model should allow us to draw conclusions which are observable in the real world.

2.1  Ingredients of a Model

Usually models in microeconomic theory are mathematical models, i.e. we will usually have a set of (in-)equations which describe the structure of the model. Within these (in-)equations we will use variables, constants and parameters.

Variables

All models need at least one variable. A variable is something that can take on different values. Very common variables in economic models include price, profit, revenue, cost, national income, consumption, investment, imports, exports, etc. As a variable can take on different values we will use symbols instead of numbers. For example p represents the price, x the quantity, m will be a personsâ€™ income, etc. As long as we use the symbols for the variables, they can take on any value. But by writing e.g. p = 3 we can assign a fix value to the variable.

There can be two types of variables in a model, endogenous and exogenous variables. An endogenous variable is determined by forces described in the model and the modelled relationships have an impact on the variable. An exogenous variable is a variable which is taken as determined by factors not discussed in this particular model. But of course both types have an influence on the outcome of our model.

Constants

A constant is a number in a model that does not change. It is a fixed number. Very often constants appear as coefficients of variables, e.g. 2p or 4x.

Parameters

To make your model more general it might be helpful to make some constants variable. E.g. to gain a higher level of generality you make the coefficients of your variables also variable, e.g. ap or bx instead of 2p or 4x. In that case this means that you can also draw conclusions from your model if the constants change their values and this implies that you model is more general.

Equations and Identities

Finally we need some equations or inequations which allow us to describe dependencies or interrelations between our variables. As an example let us assume that we have the variables price p, quantity x and revenue R. So we can identify the relationship

R(p,x) = p x

This relationship tells us that the revenue R depends on the price p and the (sold) quantity x and is calculated by multiplying price p by the quantity x.

In this example we only used variables. Now let us look at an example where we also use a constant: Assume a firm has fix costs of 100. The firm faces this cost independent of the produced quantity. Furthermore for the production of one unit of output x the firm faces costs of 25 per unit. These costs are the variable costs. Then the total costs C can be modelled as

C(x) = 25 x + 100

Now we have one variable on the right hand side of our equation, namely x and two constants, namely 25 for the variable costs per unit and 100 for the fix costs. If we now want to make our model more general, we could use parameters instead of the constants. Let us say that Cfix is the parameter for our fix costs. Then we would have

C(x) = 25 x + Cfix 

For further generalization we could also parameterize our variable unit costs cv and get

C(x) = cv x + Cfix

While the cost function with the constants will represent the cost function of one specific firm, the generalized cost function could now represent the cost function of various firms.

An important hint: The x in the term C(x) tells us, that the equation only depends on x, i.e. x is our variable. So we know that cv and Cfix have to be parameters.
